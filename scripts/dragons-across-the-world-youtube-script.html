Story Summary

Dragons appear across the world because different cultures used powerful serpent, storm, water, and treasure images to explain danger and order.

Why Dragons Look Different Across the World works as a video because it gives the creator a clear subject, a recognizable setting, and a central motif around mythology, dragon. The production should help viewers understand the main event, the background, and the emotional shape of the story before moving into platform-specific execution.

Mythology and comparative folklore setting should be presented with a quiet, mysterious, source-aware tone. Use the original archive record as the source reference, but keep factual claims, legendary motifs, and interpretive atmosphere clearly separated.

Phone Recording Alternative

휴대전화 기본 녹음만으로도 첫 영상을 완성할 수 있습니다. AI 음성을 찾는 과정에서 막힌다면 직접 녹음해도 됩니다. 완벽한 목소리보다 첫 영상을 끝까지 완성하는 경험이 더 중요합니다.

  • Record in the quietest room you can use.
  • Keep the phone about 20-30 cm from your mouth.
  • Read a little slower than normal.
  • If the full script feels long, record one Scene at a time.
  • Re-record only the mistaken part and replace it in CapCut.
  • Keep background music low enough that the voice stays clear.

Long-form Creator

Scene 1

Estimated Playback Time: 3-5 minutes

Narration: Dragons are easy to recognize, but they are not the same everywhere. In one tradition, a dragon guards treasure. In another, it brings rain. Somewhere else, it coils through rivers, tests heroes, protects sacred places, or represents chaos that must be controlled. That difference matters. The question is not only why so many cultures imagined dragons. The deeper question is why those dragons look, behave, and mean such different things.

Image Prompt: Cinematic triptych showing a cave dragon, a cloud dragon, and a river serpent in separate panels, mythic documentary style, dramatic lighting, ancient atmosphere, no cartoon style

Recommended Background Music: Mythic Ambient, Low Drums, Cinematic Mystery

Editing Guide: Hold the image for 8-10 seconds at a time. Use a slow zoom in and fade to the next scene.

Motion Prompt: Cinematic triptych showing a cave dragon, a cloud dragon, and a river serpent in separate panels, mythic documentary style, dramatic lighting, ancient atmosphere, no cartoon style. Add slow cloud movement, gentle scale or silhouette motion, and a smooth camera drift that makes the scene feel ancient and cinematic.

Sound Effect: low wind over mountains, distant thunder, deep cinematic rumble

Voice Direction: 차분한 다큐멘터리 톤으로 천천히, 문장 사이를 충분히 띄워 읽는다. 문화권이나 상징을 말할 때 과장하지 말고, 핵심 단어만 살짝 강조한다.

Camera and Motion Notes: gentle pan from left to right: 장면을 천천히 가로로 보여준다. slow push-in: 상징적인 대상 쪽으로 조금씩 다가간다.

Transition and Color Notes: 천천히 페이드 인으로 시작한다. 어두운 금색, 흐린 청색, 낮은 채도의 고대적 색감을 유지한다.

Negative Prompt: cartoon style, toy-like creature, distorted anatomy, unreadable text, random letters, duplicated objects, oversaturated colors, inconsistent lighting

Scene 2

Estimated Playback Time: 3-5 minutes

Narration: In many European stories, dragons are tied to danger, caves, hoards, saints, heroes, and battles. In many East Asian traditions, dragon figures can be connected with water, weather, imperial power, and cosmic balance. Other serpent-like beings appear in creation stories, landscape myths, and warnings about boundaries. They do not all come from one simple source. Different cultures used powerful animal and elemental images to give shape to forces people feared or respected.

Scene 3

Estimated Playback Time: 3-5 minutes

Narration: A dragon's body often tells the viewer what kind of power the story is imagining. Wings suggest the sky. Scales suggest armor. Fire suggests destruction. Water suggests rain, floods, and hidden depth. Treasure suggests danger wrapped around desire. So when we place one dragon in a cave, one in the clouds, one in the river, and one carved into a temple wall, they may share a name in modern English. But each belongs to a different symbolic world.
