A publishing checklist for making sure a mystery, folklore, or urban legend page has enough substance to help readers and deserve a place in the archive.

Thin does not simply mean short

A short page can be useful if it answers a narrow question clearly. A long page can still be thin if it repeats generic statements, avoids source status, and never gives the reader a reason to continue.

For Kyunolab Mystery Archive, thinness is about missing purpose. The page should have a specific subject, a clear angle, enough context, and a useful place in the archive.

Check the unique angle

Before publishing, ask what this page does that another page does not. Does it explain a particular motif, setting, source problem, version history, or reader question?

If the answer is only that the keyword is different, the page probably needs more work or should be folded into a stronger existing page.

Check source status before adding atmosphere

Atmosphere should sit on top of structure, not replace it. A mystery article needs to say whether the material is documented, folklore, a modern retelling, speculative interpretation, or original archive-style writing.

This is especially important for strange records that sound factual. The more realistic the detail feels, the more clearly the source limit should be named.

Check whether the page connects anywhere useful

A thin page often has nowhere to send the reader next. It may mention a category or tag, but it does not create a meaningful path into related records, guides, or source notes.

A stronger page should connect to nearby shelves: a related legend, a source-status guide, a category page, or a tag that has enough depth to matter.

Decide whether to publish, expand, or merge

If the page has a clear angle, useful context, source status, and at least a few meaningful internal paths, it can publish. If it has a good subject but not enough depth, expand it.

If it only repeats an existing page in thinner form, merge the idea into the stronger article and save the standalone URL for a topic that deserves it.

FAQ

Is a short mystery article always thin?

No. A short article can be useful if it answers a narrow reader question with specific context and clear source status.

What is the biggest warning sign of a thin page?

Generic writing. If the same paragraphs could fit dozens of topics, the page probably needs a sharper angle.

Should thin tag pages be indexed?

No. Thin tag pages can remain useful internal links, but they should not enter the sitemap until they have enough connected records and description depth.

When should a topic become a separate page?

When it has a specific reader purpose, enough context, and a natural connection to the archive structure.

Story & Source Note
