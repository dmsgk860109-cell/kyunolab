Why this matters

A growing mystery archive needs more than volume. Group Similar Legends Without Changing Old URLs helps each article earn its place by giving readers a reason to continue from one record to the next.

The goal is a quieter kind of usefulness: clear labels, stable URLs, source limits, and article paths that feel intentional rather than forced.

What to check first

Start with the reader question behind the page. If the subject is a legend, ask whether the strongest angle is origin, meaning, versions, place, or evidence limit.

Then check whether the title, deck, tags, and related links all point toward that same reader need without exaggerating the story as verified fact.

How to apply it inside Kyunolab

Use categories for the broad shelf and tags for recurring motifs. Keep related links close to genuine reader curiosity: same setting, same evidence problem, same folklore pattern, or same source status.

If a page starts serving too many jobs, split the idea into a guide and a story record instead of making one article carry every search intent.

What to avoid

Avoid keyword lists, invented certainty, thin pages, and repeated openings that make different articles feel interchangeable.

A strong page can be search-friendly and still sound like Kyunolab: calm, literary, specific, and honest about what the record can and cannot prove.

FAQ

Is this guide a rule for every page?

No. It is a practical editorial guide. Simple topics can stay simple, and deeper topics can expand when the subject needs more room.

Should SEO change the archive voice?

No. Search structure should clarify the topic, not flatten the tone or turn folklore into a factual claim.

Do guide pages replace article source notes?

No. Guides explain the method. Each article still needs its own source status and limits.

What is the safest publishing habit?

Keep the slug stable, name the source limits, avoid thin indexable pages, and make sure each related link helps a reader continue naturally.
