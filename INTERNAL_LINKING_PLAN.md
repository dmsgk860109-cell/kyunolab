# Kyunolab Internal Linking Plan

Internal links should make Kyunolab feel like a connected archive. Links must be relevant, descriptive, and useful to the reader.

## Core Principle

Every page should answer:

- Where did this story come from?
- What category does it belong to?
- What similar story should the reader open next?
- What broader archive path does this article support?

Avoid random link stuffing.

## Standard Article Link Requirements

Every article should include:

- 1 link back to its main category
- 1 to 3 contextual links inside the article body
- 3 related article links near the bottom
- Previous / Next article navigation
- Tag links where useful
- Optional cluster links if the article belongs to a series

## Link Types

### A. Category Links

Use when pointing from a specific article to a broad topic hub.

Example:

- Urban Legends -> The Vanishing Hitchhiker
- Internet Folklore -> The Backrooms
- Strange Places -> The Island No One Could Map Twice

### B. Tag Links

Use when a narrower theme exists across multiple articles.

Example:

- Roadside Folklore -> Vanishing Hitchhiker, Woman in White, Haunted Roads
- Digital Folklore -> Backrooms, Single Image Internet Legend
- Lost Places -> Strange Islands, Abandoned Towns, Mystery Forests

### C. Similar Pattern Links

Use when two stories share a narrative structure.

Example:

- Vanishing Hitchhiker -> Phantom Passenger -> Woman in White
- Strange Island -> Lost Place -> Impossible Map
- Backrooms -> Cursed Image -> Digital Labyrinth

### D. Setting-Based Links

Use when articles share a place type.

Example:

- Road legends -> haunted roads, roadside apparitions, phantom travelers
- Forest legends -> forbidden woods, forest spirits, missing paths
- Village mysteries -> bells, local records, repeated claims

### E. Reader Journey Links

Use when the next article is not identical in topic but matches the reader's likely curiosity.

Example:

- After an urban legend, suggest a folklore article with a similar emotional pattern.
- After an internet folklore entry, suggest an older legend that uses a similar image or structure.
- After a strange-place entry, suggest an unexplained mystery with a similar evidence gap.

## Related Article Logic

Each article should usually show:

1. Same category article
2. Same tag or theme article
3. Adjacent curiosity article

Example for "The Vanishing Hitchhiker":

- The Woman in White
- Haunted Roads and Phantom Passengers
- The Backrooms and the Birth of a Digital Labyrinth

## Anchor Text Rules

Use descriptive anchor text:

- Good: "the vanishing hitchhiker legend"
- Good: "internet folklore about empty rooms"
- Good: "strange-place legends about uncertain maps"

Avoid vague anchors:

- click here
- read more
- this article
- learn more

Do not repeat the exact same anchor too many times on one page.

## Visual Separation Rules

Related article titles must never run together as inline text. They must render as:

- cards
- separated list items
- clearly separated links
- previous / next blocks

Inline contextual links are acceptable inside paragraphs, but related clusters must be visually separated.

## QA Checklist

- Each article links to its main category.
- Each article has 1 to 3 contextual internal links.
- Related articles are relevant and visually separated.
- Previous / Next links do not break.
- No generic anchor text is used except UI labels.
- Links create a natural archive-style discovery flow.

## Category Architecture: Two 6-Category Groups

Kyunolab uses two visible category groups. **Modern Strange Records** contains Urban Legends, Internet Folklore, Strange Places, Unexplained Mysteries, Classic Folklore, and Modern Legends. **Mythic & Imagined Realms** contains Myths, Mythic Creatures, Lost Worlds, Strange Nature, Legendary Places, and Mythic Objects.

Use the distinction carefully: Vanishing Hitchhiker belongs in Urban Legends; Backrooms belongs in Internet Folklore; modern sighting cycles such as Loch Ness-style records belong in Modern Legends; dragons belong in Mythic Creatures; vanished cities, islands, and impossible geographies usually belong in Lost Worlds; unusual forests or natural phenomena belong in Strange Nature unless the place itself is the central legend, where Legendary Places may fit better.

Keep legacy category URLs live. Legend Origins remains a supplemental archive shelf for existing material, but it is not part of the visible 6:6 category system.
